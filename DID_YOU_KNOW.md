# StutterLab — Did You Know? FAQs

A collection of evidence-based facts organized by category, designed to surface throughout the app as educational "Did You Know?" cards.

---

## Neurological & Biological

1. **What is the "Choral Effect"?** When a person who stutters speaks in unison with someone else, they are often 100% fluent. This is due to external timing cues bypassing the defective internal timing of the basal ganglia.

2. **Do brain structures look different?** Yes, research shows less white matter volume in the left hemisphere's "speech zones" and more in the right hemisphere.

3. **Is the auditory cortex involved?** Yes, some studies suggest that people who stutter may have "faulty" monitoring of their own voice, leading the brain to "reset" the speech motor plan.

4. **What is the "Basal Ganglia" hypothesis?** It suggests the part of the brain responsible for rhythmic movement fails to provide the "go" signal for speech sounds.

5. **Can a head injury cause stuttering?** Yes, this is called neurogenic stuttering.

6. **Is there a "stuttering gene"?** Not one single gene, but several (GNPTAB, GNPTG, NAGPA) that impact how the brain processes cellular waste.

7. **Does the brain change after therapy?** Yes, fMRI studies show that successful therapy can increase activation in the left hemisphere.

8. **Is it related to Tourette's?** Both involve the basal ganglia and dopamine, but they are distinct disorders.

9. **Is it related to Parkinson's?** While both involve dopamine, there is no evidence that stuttering leads to Parkinson's.

10. **Why do people stutter more on consonants?** Consonants usually require more complex, "stopped" airflow compared to vowels.

## Childhood & Development

11. **At what age does stuttering typically start?** Between 2 and 5 years old.

12. **Is "developmental disfluency" normal?** Yes, many toddlers repeat whole words; however, repeating parts of words (b-b-ball) is a red flag.

13. **Should I wait and see?** Current research suggests "early intervention" is superior to the "wait and see" approach.

14. **Does parenting style cause stuttering?** No. This was a 1940s myth (The Diagnosogenic Theory) that has been scientifically debunked.

15. **Does "slowing down" my own speech help my child?** Yes, modeling a slower rate (not telling the child to slow down) reduces the communicative pressure.

16. **Is it okay to talk about stuttering with a child?** Yes, acknowledging it helps prevent the child from developing "shame" around speech.

17. **Can siblings cause it?** No, though a fast-paced household may increase the pressure to speak quickly.

18. **Is it more common in twins?** Yes, research indicates a slightly higher incidence in twins.

19. **Does screen time cause stuttering?** There is no scientific evidence linking screen time to the onset of stuttering.

20. **What is the recovery rate for girls vs. boys?** Girls are significantly more likely to recover spontaneously than boys.

## Mechanics & Speech Patterns

21. **What is a "Block"?** A total cessation of sound and airflow where the vocal folds are stuck together.

22. **What is a "Prolongation"?** Stretching out a sound (e.g., "Ssssssssam").

23. **What is a "Repetition"?** Repeating a sound or syllable (e.g., "b-b-b-ball").

24. **Why do some people blink or jerk their heads?** These are "secondary behaviors" — learned physical tricks used to "break out" of a stuttering block.

25. **What is "Cluttering"?** A related disorder where speech is too fast or jerky, but distinct from stuttering.

26. **Why is the first word the hardest?** Initiating airflow from a resting state requires the most coordination.

27. **Does whispering help?** Many people do not stutter while whispering because it doesn't require vocal fold vibration.

28. **Does speaking in an accent help?** Often, yes, because it forces the brain to use a different motor "template."

29. **Why is "holding breath" common?** It's a physiological reaction to the "threat" of a block, but it actually makes the block worse.

30. **Does reading aloud help?** Practice can help, but for many, reading aloud is harder because they cannot swap words.

## Social & Psychological Impact

31. **Does stuttering cause social anxiety?** Chronic stuttering significantly increases the risk of developing Social Anxiety Disorder.

32. **Is stuttering a disability?** Yes, under the ADA (Americans with Disabilities Act) and similar global laws.

33. **Do people who stutter have "quiet" personalities?** Often they appear so because they are "choosing their battles" with speech.

34. **Can you be a successful public speaker?** Yes — many prominent leaders, actors, and executives stutter or have stuttered.

35. **Does "word substitution" work?** It's a common coping mechanism, but it can lead to more mental fatigue.

36. **Is "eye contact" painful?** For some, it feels vulnerable during a block; however, maintaining it is a key goal in therapy.

37. **How should a listener react?** Wait patiently, maintain eye contact, and don't finish sentences.

38. **Can stuttering lead to depression?** The social isolation caused by severe stuttering is a known risk factor.

39. **Does "mindfulness" help?** Research shows it reduces the "fight or flight" response, making speech techniques easier to use.

40. **Are there support groups?** Yes, organizations dedicated to stuttering support are proven to improve quality of life.

---

## Treatment & Therapy

41. **Can anti-anxiety medications help?** Anxiety meds may lower stress, but they don't fix the underlying motor issue.

42. **What is "Easy Onset"?** A technique of starting a word with a very soft, breathy voice.

43. **What is "Light Contact"?** Touching the lips or tongue together very gently to prevent tension.

44. **Is the "marbles in mouth" method real?** No, that is an ancient and dangerous myth.

45. **Does hypnosis work?** There is no scientific evidence that hypnosis provides long-term fluency.

46. **What is the "Lidcombe Program"?** A parent-led behavioral program for children under 6.

47. **What is "Stuttering Modification"?** Therapy that focuses on "stuttering more fluently" rather than not stuttering at all.

48. **Can AI help?** Emerging AI tools are being used to provide real-time feedback and speech analysis.

49. **Is there a vaccine?** No, as it is not a viral or bacterial condition.

50. **How long does therapy take?** It is often an ongoing process of management rather than a "one-and-done" fix.

## Global & Cultural Facts

51. **Is it found in all cultures?** Yes, stuttering exists in every language and culture on earth.

52. **Is it more common in certain languages?** No, though "tone" languages (like Mandarin) present different rhythmic challenges.

53. **What is "World Stuttering Awareness Day"?** October 22nd.

54. **How many people worldwide stutter?** Approximately 80 million people.

55. **Do deaf people stutter?** Yes, people who use Sign Language can "stutter" in their signs (repetitive or blocked movements).

---

## Workplace & Education

56. **Can I be fired for stuttering?** No, that is illegal discrimination in most countries.

57. **Should I disclose my stutter in an interview?** Research suggests that "self-disclosure" early on reduces listener tension and increases the speaker's confidence.

58. **Are there school accommodations?** Yes (IEPs or 504 plans in the US) can allow for oral presentation alternatives.

59. **Do people who stutter earn less?** Some studies suggest a "disfluency wage gap," largely due to vocational stereotyping.

60. **Can stuttering affect my grades?** Only if a student avoids participating or is graded on "fluency" rather than content.

## Advanced Research

61. **Is the "Internal Model" faulty?** One theory suggests the brain's internal prediction of what speech should sound like is mismatched with the output.

62. **What is "Covert Stuttering"?** When someone hides their stutter so well (through word swapping) that no one knows they stutter, though the mental toll is high.

63. **Does "DAF" (Delayed Auditory Feedback) work forever?** Usually no; the brain often "habituates" to the delay, and the effect wears off.

64. **Is there a link to "apraxia"?** They are different; apraxia is a planning issue, while stuttering is an execution/timing issue.

65. **Can "transcranial magnetic stimulation" (TMS) help?** Research is ongoing to see if stimulating the left hemisphere can improve fluency.

66. **Is it related to "clonus"?** The repetitive rhythmic movements in stuttering are somewhat similar to neurological tremors.

67. **Why do I stutter less when I'm angry?** Strong emotion can sometimes "override" the standard speech pathways.

68. **Does "acting" help?** Yes, many people don't stutter when playing a character (using a different "persona").

69. **Is it a "motor" or "language" problem?** It is primarily a motor problem, though language complexity can trigger it.

70. **Does "pacing" help?** Rhythmic tapping (metronome effect) is one of the oldest known ways to induce fluency.

71. **Are there "stuttering-friendly" jobs?** Any job! People who stutter are doctors, pilots, and CEOs.

72. **Can "stretching" vocal cords help?** No, the issue is neurological, not a muscle weakness.

73. **Is it linked to "handedness"?** Old studies suggested a link to being left-handed, but modern research has found no significant correlation.

74. **Can you "stutter" in your thoughts?** Many people report a "mental block" before they even open their mouth.

75. **Is there hope for a "cure"?** While "management" is the current goal, genetic research and neuro-modulation offer hope for more targeted treatments in the future.
