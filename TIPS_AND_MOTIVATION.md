## The Neuroscience of Stuttering

84. Stuttering is a neurodevelopmental disorder — not caused by anxiety, nervousness, or parenting styles.
85. ~70% of the variance in stuttering is attributed to genetics. Specific gene mutations affect brain cell trafficking.
86. Neuroimaging shows reduced white matter integrity in the left rolandic operculum — the brain region connecting speech planning to execution.
87. People who stutter show hyperactivity in the right hemisphere — a compensatory mechanism for left-hemisphere deficits.
88. Elevated dopamine levels in the striatum disrupt speech motor timing — this is why techniques that change timing patterns (DAF, metronome) work.
89. You can talk to pets without stuttering because lack of social judgment reduces the brain's threat response. That's not a flaw — it's neuroscience.
90. Singing eliminates stuttering for most people because continuous airflow and rhythm bypass the basal ganglia timing disruption.
91. You stutter more on your own name because names can't be substituted — the brain can't fall back on word-swapping strategies.

110. Without self-disclosure, listeners are 5.5x more likely to assume you're shy and 6.6x more likely to assume you're unintelligent. One sentence changes that.

112. 72% of listeners report more positive perceptions when a speaker self-discloses about stuttering — they see courage, not weakness.
113. Self-disclosure works for everyone — even listeners who have never met someone who stutters respond more positively.

## Less Monitoring, More Fluency (Eichorn et al., 2016; Iimura et al., 2016)

116. Engaging working memory while speaking reduced stuttering-like disfluencies by 28% — your brain's automatic speech system works better with less conscious monitoring.
117. Overthinking your speech is the problem, not the solution. Research shows excessive self-monitoring interferes with natural fluency.
118. Focus on what you want to say, not how you're saying it. External focus optimizes speech production; internal focus disrupts it.
119. Your speech system is fully capable of fluency — it's about accessing automatic processes, not building new ones.
120. When attention was diverted from speech monitoring (via auditory masking), comprehension improved significantly — less self-focus frees up brainpower.
121. People who stutter and people who don't stutter showed identical patterns: everyone speaks more fluently when they stop monitoring themselves.
122. Error rates don't increase when fluency improves through distraction — you won't make more mistakes by thinking less about your speech.
